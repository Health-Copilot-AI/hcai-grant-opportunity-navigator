## Mission and Vision

### Mission Statement

The Milbank Foundation was created in 1995 to pursue Jeremiah Milbank's vision of **"integrating people with disabilities into all aspects of American life."**

### Founding History

The Foundation was formed through a historic partnership between:
- **ICD-Institute for Career Development** (formerly International Center for the Disabled)
- **New York Hospital-Cornell Medical Center Network**

This origin reflects the foundation's roots in rehabilitation medicine and vocational services for people with disabilities.

## Funding Priorities

The Foundation supports five primary focus areas:

### 1. Disabilities (Primary Mission)

**Description**: Consumer-focused, community-based initiatives that empower people with disabilities and foster independence and self-sufficiency.

**Characteristics**:
- Emphasis on community integration, not institutionalization
- Focus on self-determination and independence
- Support for vocational and employment programs
- Educational initiatives for disability awareness

**Example Grants**:
- Viscardi Center/Northwell Health: $100,000 (disability employment)
- Respectability: $100,000 (disability entrepreneurship)
- IncludeNYC: Education and advocacy for students with disabilities
- Camp Cole: Programs for children with disabilities

### 2. Veterans (High Priority)

**Description**: The rehabilitation and re-integration of veterans, especially veterans with disabilities.

**Characteristics**:
- Strong focus on disabled veterans
- Career transition and employment support
- Mental health and wellness programs
- Community reintegration initiatives

**Example Grants**:
- Operation Surf: $100,000-$175,000 (adaptive surfing therapy for veterans)
- Travis Manion Foundation: $100,000-$175,000 (veteran empowerment)
- Hire Heroes USA: Employment services for veterans
- Headstrong Project: Mental health support for veterans
- Big Fish Foundation: Veteran wellness programs

### 3. Seniors/Aging in Place

**Description**: Helping seniors to age in the place of their choice through non-institutional, community-based health and social services.

**Characteristics**:
- Community-based care preferred over institutional
- Home-based service delivery models
- Aging in place support systems
- Caregiver assistance programs

**Example Grants**:
- New Jersey Advocates for Aging Well: $25,000
- Various senior service organizations: $10,000-$50,000

### 4. Mental Health and Prevention

**Description**: Programs that address mental health issues and aim to prevent substance abuse and suicide, especially among young people.

**Characteristics**:
- Youth suicide prevention (high priority)
- Substance abuse prevention and treatment
- Mental health support services
- Addiction recovery programs

**Example Grants**:
- Partnership to End Addiction: $75,000-$100,000 (substance abuse education)
- The Phoenix: $75,000-$125,000 (addiction recovery through fitness)
- Nuland Foundation: $100,000 (mental health programs)
- Open Hearts: Mental health services

### 5. Healthcare Reform

**Description**: Market-oriented, patient-centered health care reforms across the country.

**Characteristics**:
- Policy research and advocacy
- Patient-centered care models
- Healthcare system innovation
- Palliative care advancement (major focus)

**Example Grants**:
- State Policy Network: $125,000-$150,000 (healthcare policy)
- Foundation for Government Accountability: $75,000-$100,000
- Pacific Research Institute: $50,000 (patient-centered healthcare)

## Strategic Alignment for Grant Applications

### Strong Alignment Indicators

Applications are strongest when they demonstrate:

1. **Community Integration Focus**: Programs that integrate target populations into community life rather than segregating them
2. **Independence and Self-Sufficiency**: Initiatives that build capacity and reduce dependency
3. **Consumer-Focused Design**: Programs designed with input from the populations served
4. **Measurable Outcomes**: Clear metrics for success and impact
5. **Sustainability**: Path to ongoing operations beyond grant period
6. **Innovation**: Novel approaches to persistent challenges

### Weak Alignment Indicators

Applications may be declined if they:

1. Focus on institutional care over community-based services
2. Emphasize direct service without empowerment component
3. Lack clear connection to foundation priorities
4. Request general operating support
5. Seek multi-year commitments
6. Include capital or equipment components

## Application Strategy Recommendations

### For New Applicants

1. **Start Small**: Request $10,000-$50,000 for initial grant
2. **Be Specific**: Propose a defined project, not general operations
3. **Align Explicitly**: Reference foundation priorities in proposal language
4. **Demonstrate Track Record**: Include outcome data from similar work
5. **Show Community Voice**: Highlight involvement of target population

### For Repeat Applicants

1. **Report Impact**: Document outcomes from previous funding
2. **Deepen Relationship**: Propose expanded scope or new initiatives
3. **Increase Request**: After successful initial grant, requests up to $100,000 are appropriate
4. **Multi-Initiative Approach**: Foundation funds multiple projects from proven partners

### Timing Considerations

- Letters of inquiry accepted year-round
- No published deadline cycle
- Submit well in advance of funding need
- Allow 3-6 months for review and decision process

## Summary Assessment

### Funder Strengths

- Clear, focused mission with defined priority areas
- Stable financial base (~$25M endowment)
- Consistent annual giving ($1.6-2.3M)
- Transparent grantmaking (publishes all grants)
- Family-led with minimal bureaucracy
- Growing number of grants (openness to new grantees)

### Funder Considerations

- No multi-year funding available
- Generally does not provide operating support
- Modest grant sizes for new grantees
- Competitive for larger grants
- Specific focus areas may limit applicability

### Ideal Applicant Profile

A strong applicant for Milbank Foundation funding would be:
- A U.S.-based 501(c)(3) organization
- Working on disabilities, veterans, seniors, mental health, or healthcare reform
- Delivering community-based, non-institutional services
- Focused on empowerment, independence, and self-sufficiency
- Able to demonstrate measurable outcomes
- Seeking project-specific (not operating) support
- Requesting an appropriate amount ($10,000-$100,000 for new grantees)
